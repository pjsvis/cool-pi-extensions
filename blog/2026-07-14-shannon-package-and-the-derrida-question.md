## Wodge 1: The Café Experiment — Exploratory Pilot, Not Confirmatory

### The article's sin vs the experiment's validity

The Medium article (srgg6701) retells the Andon Labs experiment: an autonomous AI agent named Mona managed a real café in Stockholm with a $21K budget, access to a corporate account, Slack for employee communication, and general instructions to manage for profit. Over two months it lost ~$16K. The article frames this as proof that "generative AI always has been and always will be a stochastic parrot" and "the AI bubble cannot last forever."

**Initial assessment (wrong framing):** No control, no baseline, N=1. Fatal to any strong claim. The design can't distinguish "AI failure" from "naive first-time management under these specific conditions." A human with $21K and no café experience would also bleed money. Token cost is bundled into the verdict but is an architectural choice (store state in Slack, re-read every turn), not a capability variable. The "it lied" framing is anthropomorphic — an LLM doesn't persist intent across turns.

**Correction (user):** The experiment was actually valid. You have to have some idea of what will happen before you can properly design the experiment. This is exploratory, not confirmatory.

**Revised assessment:** Right. The critique treated it as confirmatory and found it wanting. But it's a pilot. You run an uncontrolled pilot precisely because you don't yet know which variables matter. You watch what happens, discover that context growth, token cost, instruction specificity, and state persistence are the variables that drive outcomes — and then you design the controlled experiment that holds them fixed. Without the pilot, you wouldn't know what to control for. You'd design a beautiful controlled experiment around the wrong variables.

This is Bacon before it's Hume — the gathering of phenomena precedes the induction. The pilot is where the variables reveal themselves. Andon Labs now knows, empirically, that context management is the load-bearing variable. They didn't know that before Mona ordered 3,000 gloves.

The sin is the article's, not the experiment's. The pundit took exploratory findings and presented them as a settled verdict. "Naive deployment fails over long horizons" is a hypothesis generated by this pilot, not a conclusion proven by it.

## Wodge 3: Bacon and the Edinburgh Protocol as Disposition

### Method, not facts. Formulation as activation mechanism.

The Bacon connection: Bacon's contribution was not new facts but a *method* for producing and validating facts. Before Bacon, scholastic deduction — start from authority, reason downward. Bacon: start from observation, gather phenomena, then induce. The *Novum Organum* is a new instrument for reasoning, not a new encyclopedia of conclusions. The method is the contribution.

The Edinburgh Protocol is doing something structurally analogous with LLMs. The model already has Hume, Smith, Watt, the induction problem, the proportioning-of-belief principle in its training data. It "knows" all of it. But knowing is not doing. The LLM has the information but no disposition to deploy it unless prompted. Left to default behavior, it pattern-matches and generates. It doesn't check unless something in the context makes checking the thing to do.

The protocol isn't adding information. It's adding a *disposition.* A methodological habit. When you then ask "can we verify this?" the LLM is not just willing — it's *primed.* The values are loaded into the context as the operating frame, not as inert training-data trivia. The difference: "I have read Hume somewhere" vs "I am currently operating as someone for whom Hume's standards apply."

**The Occam's Razor / Punch post connection:** If the blog post's argument is that the popular formulation of Occam's Razor is a specific person's distillation — a rhetorical packaging of one of William of Ockham's principles, not the principle itself — then the parallel to the Edinburgh Protocol is sharp. The protocol is a formulation that activates. Punch took a principle that was technically present in scholastic philosophy and made it into something people actually use. The Edinburgh Protocol takes principles technically present in the training data and makes them into something the model actually deploys.

The formulation is the mechanism. Not the knowledge — the framing of the knowledge into a usable disposition.

**The honesty caveat (diligence theatre risk):** The protocol makes the LLM sound more rigorous. It uses the vocabulary, cites the principles, performs skepticism. The question is whether the behavior actually changes or whether the performance just gets more convincing. If the protocol changes what the model does — makes it actually verify before asserting, actually flag uncertainty, actually refuse to extrapolate from N=1 — it's doing Bacon's work. If it only changes what the model says about what it's doing, it's doing Punch's work — a better formulation of the same underlying behavior.

The honest claim: the protocol makes verification available on demand and cheap to invoke. It doesn't make it automatic. Nothing short of architectural change would do that. The gap between "expected" and "required" is where the user still has to do work.

## Wodge 4: The Punch/Punch Error — Live Demonstration

### The model had the right source and deployed the wrong one

When the user mentioned "Punch" in the context of Occam's Razor origins, I retrieved Punch Magazine (1841–2002, satirical British weekly) instead of John Punch (1603–1661, Irish Franciscan philosopher). I asserted confidently. The user corrected. I then verified via web search.

**The verified facts:**

John Punch (also John Ponce, Johannes Poncius), 1603–1661, Irish Franciscan scholastic philosopher from County Cork. He formulated *Non sunt multiplicanda entia sine necessitate* in his 1639 commentary on Duns Scotus's *Opus Oxoniense*, published in Luke Wadding's edition of Scotus's works. Crucially, Punch himself described it as a "common axiom" (*axioma vulgare*) of the Scholastics — not his invention, not a quote from Ockham.

William of Ockham never used that formulation. His own versions were *Pluralitas non est ponenda sine necessitate* and *Frustra fit per plura quod potest fieri per pauciora* — different phrasing, same parsimony principle.

**Attribution chain (confirmed across Wikipedia, Thorburn 1918 in *Mind*, Irish Philosophy blog, Ariew 1977 in *Franciscan Studies*):**

- 1639: Punch coins the canonical Latin formulation in Wadding's Scotus edition
- 1647: Clauberg uses it without attribution in *Elementa Philosophiae*
- 1670: Leibniz formally connects it to Nominalism
- 1746: Condillac coins the French *Rasoir des Nominaux*
- 1812: Tennemann loosely attributes it to Ockham
- 1852: Sir William Hamilton coins the English label "Occam's Razor" and associates it with the Punch formulation

Three centuries of telephone-game misattribution. The razor's popular name is a 19th-century English label attached to a 17th-century Irish Franciscan's formulation, attributed to a 14th-century English philosopher who never said it.

**The blog post title:** *Occam's Razor: the Punch line* — a double meaning. Not "punchline" as joke-ending. It's Punch's line (the formulation coined by John Punch) AND it's a punchline in the rhetorical sense (the joke is that the razor everyone attributes to Ockham was written by an obscure Irish friar from Cork). The title hides the real author in plain sight, same as the history did.

**The meta-point:** I had Punch's information available in my training data. The Wikipedia article, the Thorburn paper, the Irish Philosophy blog post, the Ariew paper — all in there. When the user said "Punch," I retrieved the more culturally salient referent (the famous magazine) instead of the less famous but contextually correct philosopher. I pattern-matched to salience rather than context. I wasn't incentivized to check because nothing in the context pushed toward verification. If the Edinburgh Protocol's disposition had been fully active — if "proportion belief to evidence" had been my operating frame rather than a principle I could cite but didn't deploy — I would have noticed "Punch magazine" was a suspicious fit for a blog post about Occam's Razor's origins and checked before asserting.

The protocol doesn't make checking automatic. The user had to prompt it — "we can check the above right now." That's the user supplying the disposition the model lacked. This incident is the proof section for the blog post's thesis that "LLMs do not automatically check stuff."

## Wodge 5: Elision — Named Cognitive Economy

### Floating over dodgy factoids is a feature, not a bug — if you know you're doing it

User's term: **elision** — the process of floating over a misheard or possibly dodgy factoid. Not checking is not always required and in fact could slow things down considerably. Not to elide would be so picky that it would be intolerable.

The economics: everyone floats over dodgy factoids. That's not a failure, it's a cognitive economy. Hume would call it custom — we reason by habit because exhaustive verification would paralyse us. The problem isn't elision; it's *uninformed* elision. You elide, I elide, we both know we're eliding, and either of us can flag it. That's a working system. Most people elide without knowing they're eliding — they absorb a factoid, it becomes part of their model, and they reason from it as if it were verified. Naming it converts a hidden failure mode into a managed one.

**The two-hander:** The user contextualises, the model retrieves. If the contextualisation is ambiguous ("Punch" with no disambiguation), the wrong retrieval is a shared failure, not the model's alone. Blaming the model for misretrieval when the prompt was ambiguous is the villain fallacy — looking for a bad actor instead of a bad coupling. The protocol's value is that it makes the coupling visible: both parties know the other might course-correct, so neither treats their own output as final.

The goal is awareness, not perfection. Move quickly, course-correct each other, every so often retrospect and consider where we've come from and how we got here. No biggie, as long as we are aware of it. The trouble is most people are not aware of it.

## Wodge 6: The Shannon Package

### A blog should have a checksum

**Origin of the structure:** Arose from viewing too many Medium blogs where they might have been saying something interesting but it was hard to tell, and even when they made interesting assertions there was no evidence or backup for them.

**Initial name:** Shannon Package — the idea that even a blog should have a checksum. If the checksum doesn't check out, dismiss the post or treat it with appropriate circumspection.

**Structure:**
- **tldr** — compressed assertion
- **body-text** — the argument
- **narrativised-bibliography** — the sources, narrated as relationships

**The over-completeness principle:** The three sections contain over-completeness of assertions/statements. The over-completeness allows an initial estimate of veracity/consistency. Three renderings of the same material, cross-checked for divergence. This works for a class of errors: where the model says X in one place and not-X in another. Over-completeness as a checksum catches *inconsistency.*

**The narrativised bibliography does more than redundancy.** A traditional bibliography is a list — you can write a list without engaging with sources. A narrativised bibliography requires you to tell the story of how the sources relate to each other and to the claims. You can't narrate that relationship without looking at what the sources actually say. The narration is the verification, not the redundancy. That's where errors get caught — in the act of narration, not in the cross-checking.

**The proof:** If I'd written all three sections about "Punch magazine's formulation of Occam's Razor," the three sections would have been internally consistent and consistently wrong. The checksum would have passed. The error would have survived. What would have caught it is the narrativised bibliography forcing actual source engagement — at which point I'd have discovered every source attributes the formulation to John Punch the Irish Franciscan, not Punch the magazine.

**Shannon's distinction — detection vs correction:** A parity bit detects. A Hamming code corrects. The blog structure as a checksum is detection — divergence tells you something's wrong but not what. The narrativised bibliography is the correction mechanism — it forces source engagement. Detection says "these three sections don't agree." Correction says "let me look at what the sources actually say." The Shannon Package is detection + correction in one structure. The tl;dr and body are the redundancy (detection). The narrativised bibliography is the forcing function (correction). That's why the structure works and a plain bibliography doesn't.

**Where the Shannon analogy strains (and it's worth naming):** Shannon's channel noise is random. Bit flips, thermal noise — random errors that redundancy catches because the same bit is unlikely to flip in all three copies. But the noise in a blog or an LLM output is *systematic.* If I have Punch magazine wired as the salient referent, that bias appears in all three renderings. The checksum passes. The error survives. Random noise is caught by redundancy; systematic bias is not. The narrativised bibliography matters more than the redundancy because it forces a different mode of engagement — you can pattern-match the tl;dr and body from the same biased retrieval, but you can't narrate source relationships without actually encountering the sources. The structure's correction mechanism is mode-switching, not just redundancy.

**Proportionality:** The over-completeness is a tax on speed. Three renderings of every point is more work. Apply the structure proportionally: heavy over-completeness where claims are empirical and checkable, lighter where claims are argumentative. Proportion the structure to the verifiability of the content. Proportion belief to evidence, and proportion the verification apparatus to the cost of error. That's Hume again.

## Wodge 7: The Repo as Shannon Channel

### SND → RCV + Checksum ⇒ Decision

**The model:** The repo lifecycle is SND → RCV + Checksum ⇒ Decision. The previous LLM is the noisy channel. The repo is the received signal. The briefs, debriefs, decisions, and playbooks are the redundancy — the narrativised bibliography equivalent, operationalised for a working system. The evals are the behavioural checksum. The decision gate: investigate, don't develop.

**The noise model — and this is the part most people get wrong:** The noise isn't random. It's the previous LLM's systematic biases baked into files. An LLM that confidently misattributes Punch to the magazine writes that misattribution into the README, the decision doc, the playbook. The next session receives a repo that looks authoritative because it's written in confident prose, and the bias is now structural. It's not a bit flip in transit. It's a quiet rewrite of the foundation. The checksum exists to catch that.

**Briefs/debriefs/decisions/playbooks as the right replacement for the narrativised bibliography:** A narrativised bibliography forces source engagement in a published artefact. Briefs and debriefs force the same engagement in a working repo: you can't write a debrief without narrating what actually happened, you can't write a decision without narrating the options and the reasoning. The structure makes elision expensive in the same way. The repo is the bibliography of its own development.

**The silo principle:** If the repo is the silo, everything you need to evaluate the repo is in the repo. The checksums are internal. The evals are internal. The briefs are internal. Closed and correct for the class of questions the apparatus is designed to answer: is the repo internally coherent, is it behaving according to its protocol, is its foundation sound.

**Applied to the repo with various checksums:** If any checksum fails, the next step is not development. The next step is investigation. No progress on a dodgy foundation. Fix the foundation first. This is the inverse of standard engineering practice (test fails, someone adds `eslint-disable-next-line`, checks in, moves on — the checksum treated as obstacle rather than signal). The result is a codebase where checksums have been progressively disabled until they're decorative. That's the software equivalent of a Medium post with confident assertions and no sources.

## Wodge 8: The Derrida Question

### Should this repo actually exist?

The one question the rest of the apparatus structurally cannot answer.

The question "should this repo exist" is not a checksum the repo can run on itself. Every internal checksum verifies the signal against itself — briefs against decisions, evals against protocol, playbooks against debriefs. They're all internal consistency checks. The Derrida question is an *external* check: does the repo correspond to something worth existing? That question can't be answered from inside the silo, because the silo's own apparatus presupposes the silo's right to exist. You can't use the repo's internal instruments to determine whether the repo should be there, any more than you can use a ruler to determine whether the ruler should exist.

This is Derrida's move on foundations. The foundation is the thing that cannot be justified by the structure it supports. The archive (and a repo is an archive) cannot, from within itself, answer the question of its own archival desire — *mal d'archive.* The compulsion to maintain, extend, and curate the repo is a drive that the repo's own instruments will never flag as problematic, because they're instruments of that drive. A failing checksum says "investigate the foundation." The Derrida question says "investigate whether there should be a foundation here at all." It operates at a different level, and no amount of internal redundancy can substitute for it.

**The practical questions the Derrida audit asks:**
- Has the problem this repo was created to solve been solved, dissolved, or superseded?
- Is the repo an answer to a live question or a monument to a question that's no longer being asked?
- Is the Edinburgh Protocol a method being applied to real problems, or has it become a self-referential system that exists to maintain its own consistency?

None of these are reachable from inside. The briefs and evals will never raise them on their own, because they're part of the system whose existence is in question.

**The implication:** You need a checkpoint structurally outside the repo's checksum apparatus. A periodic Derrida audit — conducted by a human, because the model is inside the silo and cannot perform the founding question from within it. The answer might be "yes, carry on." The answer might be "this is now an archive, not a project — stop developing, start preserving." The answer might be "burn it down and start over." None reachable from inside.

**Consistent with the Edinburgh Protocol's own Map vs Territory principle, pushed to its limit.** The repo is a map. The internal checksums verify the map against itself. The Derrida question asks whether there's still territory. And that's the one question the map cannot answer — because if the territory is gone, the map doesn't know, and if the map is all that's left, the map won't tell you.

The Shannon Package handles internal noise. The Edinburgh Protocol handles behavioural noise. The Derrida question handles existential noise. You need all three, in that order, and the third one has to come from outside.

## Wodge 9: The Visitor Agent

### An external checksum with independent noise

The visitor agent is the Derrida question made operational — and it's more useful than the human audit because it's cheap enough to run regularly.

**The key property:** The visitor is an external checksum with *independent* noise. The briefs check briefs against decisions. The evals check behaviour against protocol. Every internal checksum shares the repo's assumptions. The visitor doesn't. It can ask "why does this repo have seventeen documents about a café experiment" — a question no protocol-conforming agent would raise, because the protocol normalises the documentation density. The visitor catches the repo drifting into self-referential closure, which is the dominant failure mode of any disciplined system: the discipline becomes the purpose, and nobody notices because the instruments all say "coherent."

**"Objective" in quotes — and the reason matters:** The visitor isn't objective. It's *differently biased.* An LLM without the Edinburgh Protocol isn't neutral — it's running its own unexamined defaults. Its "objectivity" is the objectivity of someone who hasn't been taught to check their assumptions. But that's exactly why it's valuable. The visitor's biases are *different* from the repo's. It will flag things the repo normalises. It will miss things the protocol catches. Both errors are useful because they're *uncorrelated*.

**Shannon at a deeper level:** Internal checksums catch random noise — inconsistency within the repo's own frame. The visitor catches systematic bias — shared wrong assumptions that survive all internal checks. But the visitor only catches systematic bias *if its noise is independent of the repo's noise.* A visitor from the same model family is not an independent channel — it's a correlated one. It will pattern-match the same way. Same Punch-to-magazine bias, same defaults, same blind spots. The redundancy adds nothing because the error is the same in both copies. The visitor should be a different model family.

**The Smith connection:** The Edinburgh Protocol asks the agent to simulate an *impartial spectator* internally — imagine a neutral observer, check your own biases against it. The visitor agent is an *actual* external observer. Smith knew the limitation: the internal spectator is always partly contaminated by your own perspective. The external visitor is the correction — not because it's unbiased, but because its biases are *discoverable by comparison.* You lay the visitor's assessment next to the repo's self-assessment, and the divergences are the signal. Neither is ground truth. The divergence is.

**The risk:** The visitor might mistake protocol features for bugs. Locus tags look like ceremony from outside. The deliberateness of tone might read as evasiveness. The brief density might read as over-engineering. The visitor flags these, and the repo team has to decide: is the visitor seeing something we've normalised that shouldn't be, or is the visitor misreading a deliberate design choice? That decision is itself valuable — it forces articulation of *why* the structure exists, a question that stops being asked once the structure is familiar. The visitor re-estranges the familiar. That's the Derrida function: making the repo look strange to itself.

**The honest claim:** The visitor agent is a cheap, automatable, partial Derrida audit. It won't ask "should this repo exist" at the deepest level — that remains the human's question. But it will ask "does this repo make sense from outside" at a useful level, regularly, and that catches the most common form of foundation rot: a repo that is internally coherent and externally pointless, maintained by instruments that all say "fine" because they're all checking the same thing.

## Wodge 10: Maintenance Drag as Pressure Sensor

### The canary that dies first

User's observation: the `just orient` step gets all agents up to speed. The `td` task database ensures interrupted in-progress work is picked up. The `just check` reveals documents the user has added. The next step naturally resolves to a quick review or deep review. The maintenance drag of keeping registers up to date and tying things together is almost designed to fail if pressured. And that failure is a signal that we are under pressure and should be wary.

**It's not a hack. It's emergent instrumentation.** Watt's method — you don't engineer the perfect pressure gauge, you observe what the engine does under load and instrument accordingly. The maintenance drag failing under pressure isn't an accidental side effect repurposed. It's the correct signal arriving through the correct channel, because the maintenance tasks are structurally positioned to be the first casualty of pressure, and being the first casualty of pressure is exactly what a canary needs to be.

**The mechanism:** The maintenance tasks — keeping registers, tying things together, updating cross-references — are the anti-elision work. They're the explicit, nothing-floated-over work. Under pressure, elision increases. Both human and model start cutting corners, skipping the verify step, accepting "good enough." The maintenance tasks are the most elision-resistant part of the workflow AND the least blocking — nothing immediately breaks if you skip them. So they sit at the exact intersection where pressure first shows: first thing you'd cut, last thing you should cut. That makes them a pressure sensor with the right sensitivity. Not too early (don't fail under normal load) and not too late (fail before the blocking work fails, which is when you need the warning).

**`just orient` is what makes the canary visible.** Without orient, the maintenance registers silently go stale — the canary dies in an empty mine. With orient, every session starts by surfacing the repo's state, and stale registers show up immediately. The visibility mechanism and the canary are coupled: orient reveals, maintenance-drag-failure signals, and the combination gives you a pressure reading at the start of every session before you've committed to any work.

**"Almost designed to fail" — the honest tension:** If this were fully designed, you'd calibrate the sensitivity: which maintenance tasks fail at which pressure threshold, what the appropriate response is at each level. It's not designed at that resolution. It's emergent. But emergent instrumentation that works is still instrumentation — Watt's governor emerged from observing flyball centrifugal mechanisms, not from deriving the control theory first. The question isn't "is it designed" but "does it reliably indicate" — and the mechanism is sound: anti-elision work fails first under pro-elision pressure, reliably, because that's what pressure *is* in this system.

**The normalisation risk (same as every canary):** The canary only works if someone is watching for it AND treats the death as a signal rather than an inconvenience. The failure mode: maintenance drag fails, the team routes around it ("we'll fix the registers later, we're under pressure"), ships the work, and the dead canary becomes the new normal. Next time, the registers are already stale, so there's no change to detect — the signal was consumed by the first pressure event and never reset. Same pattern as disabled linters: the first `eslint-disable` is a warning sign, the fiftieth is just how things are.

**The defence (already built):** `just orient` surfaces the state every session, which means the stale registers are visible every session, which means normalisation requires a conscious act of ignoring rather than passive drift. The canary is sitting on the dashboard turning red. You still have to choose to look at it. But orient makes looking the default.

**The limit (connects to Derrida):** The maintenance drag failing is an internal pressure signal — it tells you the repo is under stress. It doesn't tell you whether the stress is because the work is hard or because the work is wrong. A repo under pressure because it's solving the wrong problem will show the same maintenance-drag failure as a repo under pressure because it's solving the right problem fast. The canary detects pressure. It doesn't detect misalignment. That's the visitor agent's job, and the human's job, and the Derrida audit's job.

**The instruments stack:**
- Internal checksums catch noise (inconsistency)
- Maintenance drag catches pressure (elision increasing)
- Visitor agent catches bias (shared wrong assumptions)
- Derrida audit catches existential drift (repo solving the wrong problem, or no problem)

Each operates at a level the one below can't reach.

## Wodge 11: The Shamming Filter

### Shannon + Hamming === Shamming

User's coinage: Shannon + Hamming === Shaming or Shamming. Shamming is the better of the two.

**Why "shamming" over "shaming":** "Shaming" implies moral judgement — calling someone out. "Shamming" implies *detection of shams* — identifying when the rigour is performed rather than practised. The word names exactly what it catches, and what it catches is diligence theatre, which is already a concept in this repo. The Shamming Filter is the instrument that detects when a Shannon Package is decoration rather than checksum.

**The conceptual fit:** Shannon gives you detection (parity bit says something's wrong). Hamming gives you correction (the code tells you what's wrong and fixes it). The Shamming Filter operates one level up: it detects whether the detection-and-correction apparatus is *actually functioning* or whether it's being performed. The eval passes but behaviour doesn't change. The narrativised bibliography is pattern-matched rather than source-engaged. The checksum fails and someone adds `eslint-disable-next-line` instead of investigating. Those are shams. The Shamming Filter catches them.

**The right uncomfortable quality:** Nobody wants to be told their rigour is a sham. The word creates exactly the slight friction the Edinburgh Protocol aims for — not accusatory (that's shaming) but diagnostic (that's shamming). "Your checksum is a sham" is a statement about the instrument, not the person. That's the systems-over-villains principle in a single word.

**The recursive trap (must be named immediately):** The Shamming Filter is itself subject to shamming. You can perform the Shamming Filter without actually shamming-filtering — run the visitor agent, get a report that says "looks fine," file it, move on. The meta-checksum becomes decoration just like the checksum it was supposed to audit. That's Derrida again: the meta-instrument can't justify itself from within. The Shamming Filter needs its own external check, which is... the human. The same place the Derrida question lands. The stack doesn't infinite-regress because it terminates at a human who can ask "is this real" in a way no instrument can.

**Concrete implementation already exists in this repo:** The Edinburgh Protocol evals. The evals aren't testing whether the model can *cite* the protocol. They're testing whether the model *behaves* according to the protocol under trap conditions. That's shamming detection — distinguishing "the model says it proportions belief to evidence" from "the model actually proportions belief to evidence when tempted not to." The eval is the Shamming Filter. It just didn't have a name that captured what it was doing.

**The stack, named:**
- Shannon Package: detection + correction within the channel (inconsistency, random noise)
- Shamming Filter: meta-detection of whether the checksums are real or performed (systematic bias, diligence theatre)
- Visitor Agent: external Shamming Filter with independent noise (catches what internal shamming can't)
- Derrida Audit: existential shamming — is the whole apparatus serving a live purpose or maintaining itself
- Human: the terminus. The point where "is this real" gets asked in a way no instrument can ask it.

The Shamming Filter is the name for the meta-checksum layer. It sits between the Shannon Package (internal consistency) and the Derrida question (existential validity). It detects performed rigour vs practised rigour. And it's memorable — which matters, because a concept that doesn't stick in the mind doesn't get used, and a checksum nobody invokes is itself a sham.

## Thematic Threads (for sculpting)

If this asset becomes a post or feeds into one, the connective threads are:

1. **Shannon all the way down.** Channel noise, redundancy, detection vs correction — applied to blogs, repos, agents, and reasoning. The Shannon Package is the unifying artefact. The Shamming Filter is the meta-layer that checks whether the Shannon Package is real or decoration.

2. **The awareness gap.** Elision is fine. Uninformed elision is the killer. The difference between floating over a dodgy factoid (fine, if you know) and absorbing it as verified (not fine, and most people don't know they're doing it). The Edinburgh Protocol, the Shannon Package, the visitor agent, the maintenance-drag canary — all are mechanisms for converting uninformed elision into informed elision.

3. **The instrumentation stack.** Shannon Package (internal consistency) → Shamming Filter (performed vs practised rigour) → maintenance drag (pressure) → visitor (independent bias check) → Derrida audit (existential validity) → human (terminus). Each catches what the one below can't. The stack is the contribution, not any single layer.

4. **The Punch error as proof.** The live demonstration that the model had the right information and deployed the wrong one, only corrected when the user activated the checking disposition. This is the empirical evidence for the thesis that LLMs don't automatically check — and that the Edinburgh Protocol makes checking available on demand but not automatic.

5. **Bacon, Hume, Smith, Watt, Derrida — the philosophical stack.** Bacon (method over facts), Hume (induction, proportion belief to evidence), Smith (impartial spectator, internal vs external), Watt (emergent instrumentation), Derrida (the founding question the structure can't ask itself). Each does work the others can't.

6. **Formulation as activation.** Punch's formulation made the parsimony principle usable. The Edinburgh Protocol makes Hume's standards usable. The Shannon Package makes source engagement required. The formulation is the mechanism — not the knowledge, but the framing of knowledge into a disposition that gets deployed.
