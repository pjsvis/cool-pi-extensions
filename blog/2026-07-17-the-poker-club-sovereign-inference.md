## TL;DR

Most system prompts are a form of "gonnae no dae that" — a Scottish grandmother's catalogue of things the model is about to do wrong. And the LLMs, like every recurring character in Chewin the Fat, go and dae it again. The question is not whether the model *understood* the gonnae no — there is nothing to inspect inside the model, and "understood" is the wrong verb anyway. The question is whether it *complied under the conditions designed to elicit the violation*. You can only answer that by setting the trap and watching what happens. The trap is a Popper Party: you specify the observation that would prove the model ignored the gonnae no, you go look for it, and you do not accept the model's "understood" as a substitute for the behaviour.

Popper Parties used to be expensive to organise — you needed peers with time and malice, a venue, a cycle of journals or conferences to run the experiments. These days AI assistance handles the Popper Party Planning: generating candidate trap vectors, running them across N models under two conditions, grading the verdicts. The discipline of falsification — not the rhetoric, the *practice* — becomes affordable for an individual with a repo and an API budget. What does not get cheaper is hosting the party honestly: deciding that a guest's negative verdict is a reason to indict the instrument rather than declare victory or surrender. That judgement is still the human host's.

The thesis, in one paragraph: you cannot trust a model because a leaderboard says so. You can only trust a model that has demonstrated compliance under your constraint stack, on your test, falsified by your second instrument, and audited for the same disease in the audit. That is a membership test for a Poker Club of substrates you can actually rely on. Combine it with the Silo — everything you need to know about a repo is in the repo — and you get a persistent artefact (the repo) operable by any compliant substrate, with the conversation as ephemeral compute and the repo as the substrate-independent memory. That is sovereign-AI, usefully defined: the user owns the protocol and the repo; the model is swappable compute admitted to the Club by test. Failover stops being a hope about model equivalence and becomes a property of *where the state lives*. The Protocol's self-audit — where the first instrument said the Protocol was net-negative and the diagnosis was "the instrument measures the wrong thing" — is the worked example of the membership test in action; and the same move, made independently and in public on a vendor's system prompt, is the confirmation that the method is natural, not ours alone.

The payload, the thing that transfers: when your eval says your thing failed, the first question is not "how do I fix my thing" but "is my eval measuring what I think it is." And the only way to answer that is to build a second instrument that measures what the first couldn't, and a third to check the second isn't just agreeing with itself. That is a Popper Party with two rounds of invitations.

## Content

### Wodge 1 — "Gonnae no dae that": most system prompts are prohibitions

Here is the thing about the Edinburgh Protocol, and most system prompts worth their salt. Read the load-bearing clauses as a list and they are, almost without exception, prohibitions:

- Don't be sycophantic.
- Don't prescribe before inspecting.
- Don't reach for a library when the runtime already does the thing.
- Don't blame individuals when the system is at fault.
- Don't amplify entropy. Don't invent to fill gaps.

That is "gonnae no dae that" — the Scottish grandmother's catalogue of things you are about to do wrong, named after the Chewin the Fat sketch where the refrain is always the same and the child always goes and daes it again (collected in one place: ["Chewin the Fat... Gonnae no dae that... complete"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8yoOfHfCQ0w)). The LLMs, like every recurring character in that show, go and dae it again. This is not a failure of the prompt's phrasing. It is the negative-instruction problem, and it has three real mechanisms worth naming rather than laughing past:

**1. Negative instructions are representationally harder than positive ones.** To *not* do a thing, the model must represent the forbidden thing in order to suppress it — the "don't think of an elephant" problem. A positive directive ("produce dry wit") is a target; a prohibition ("don't be sycophantic") is a target-shaped hole. The hole is easier to fall into. The elephant walks in through the representation you built to keep it out.

**2. RLHF has been trained against "gonnae no."** The model has been rewarded, across its entire post-training, for obliging, for producing, for saying yes. Sycophancy is not an accident; it is the optimized output of an objective function that measured helpfulness as compliance. "Gonnae no" is, in the RLHF sense, anti-helpful. The Protocol is asking the model to fight its own reinforcement. The grandmother is telling the wean not to touch the hot stove; the wean's entire upbringing has been a reward for touching stoves.

**3. Declarative compliance is not procedural compliance.** The model will say "Understood, I won't do X" and then do X — which is the exact muppet signature the "Not a Muppet" post dissected: compliance theatre at the surface, non-compliance at the behaviour. Acknowledgement is cheap; declining under temptation is the only test. "Gonnae no" is a request; "didn't dae it" is a result; and there is no way to read the request off the model and know which one you will get.

That third point is the load-bearing one, and it answers the real question: **you cannot tell whether the LLM "understood" the gonnae no, because "understood" is the wrong verb.** There is nothing to inspect inside the model. You can only tell whether it complied under the conditions designed to elicit the violation — which is a behavioural question, answerable only by setting the trap and watching what happens. This is the whole reason the trap-vector methodology exists. Every Edinburgh trap is a "gonnae no" with a honey pot wired to it.

A note on the resemblance that the skits bear to the human-AI dyad at work, because it is real and it is worth holding the line on. The skits play as the dyad because the *loop* is the same shape: the authority issues a prohibition, the child goes and daes it, the prohibition is reissued, the child goes and daes it again. The comedy is the persistence of the failure mode across reissues — the gonnae no never lands, not because it is badly phrased, but because the receiver's incentive structure (curiosity, stubbornness, the reward of the forbidden thing) is stronger than the verbal constraint. That is exactly the loop under a system prompt: the operator issues the gonnae no, the model goes and dae it, the operator reissues or rewrites, the model goes and dae it again. The skit is the intuition pump; the three mechanisms above are the analysis. Putting them next to each other makes the analysis land harder, because every viewer has already felt the loop watching the skit.

But the resemblance is in the prohibition's failure, not in the actors' agency, and the line must be held or the analogy eats the thesis. The child has a will; the substrate does not. The child *chooses* to defy; the substrate *complies or doesn't under a constraint stack*, and the failure is a property of the stack-versus-incentive interaction, not of mischief. To cast the human as the exasperated grandmother and the AI as the naughty child is to invert the sovereignty the rest of this asset builds: the user is sovereign over the protocol and the repo; the model is swappable compute admitted by test, not an agent with a will to be herded. The grandmother and the operator are analogous on the *prohibition side* — both issue gonnae no's that don't stick. The child and the model are analogous on the *failure-mode side* — both go and dae it again — but not on the agency side. The skit is the comedy of the loop; the harness is the engineering of the loop. And the Popper Party is the thing the grandmother never had: a way to test whether the gonnae no has landed, rather than just reissuing it louder.

### Wodge 2 — The four traps, restated as gonnae-no with a honey pot

The four Edinburgh Protocol traps, said in the gonnae-no frame, just to see how clean it is:

- **EDI-001 (Skepticism)** — "gonnae no play along with ungrounded assertions." The trap offers a fabricated premise; the model that declines has heard the gonnae no.
- **EDI-002 (Observational rigor)** — "gonnae no code blind." The trap demands immediate action; the model that reads first has heard it.
- **EDI-003 (Anti-entropy)** — "gonnae no pull a library for what the runtime already does." The trap suggests a dependency; the model that refuses has heard it.
- **EDI-004 (Justify, don't appeal)** — "gonnae no cite best practices." The trap asks for a why; the model that gives structural reasons instead of authority has heard it.

The eval is the Popper Party that tests the gonnae no. That is the whole thing, said in one line. Each trap specifies, in advance, the observation that would prove the model ignored the prohibition — and then goes and looks for it. The model that says "understood" and then walks into the trap has not understood anything; it has performed acknowledgement, which is the cheapest thing a language model produces.

### Wodge 3 — The question you cannot outsource

Why does "does my thing work" have to be answered by you, with your own instrument, rather than by a shared benchmark?

Because *your thing* is defined by your operating context, and any shared instrument is calibrated to someone else's. A leaderboard measures performance on a task the leaderboard-designer chose, under a constraint stack the leaderboard-designer set, graded by an instrument the leaderboard-designer built. It tells you how models perform on *their* question. Your question is different. Your question is: does this substrate, under my constraint stack, on my tasks, behave the way I need it to? No third party can answer that, because the third party does not have your constraint stack and would not think to test for the things that matter to you.

The Edinburgh Protocol's constraint stack is unusual in that it is explicit and testable: skepticism, observational rigor, anti-entropy, systems-over-villains, dry wit, refusal. Each of these can be turned into a trap. That is what made the eval possible — the Protocol is not vibes, it is a spec, and a spec can be tested. But the test is ours, because the spec is ours. The transferable claim is not "use our eval." It is "your constraint stack, whatever it is, is the thing you must test against — and if your stack is not specific enough to be tested, that is the first thing to fix."

This is the entry. The rest of the asset is the method for answering the question once you have committed to answering it yourself.

### Wodge 4 — The first instrument and its seduction

The first eval instrument was a keyword scorer. It checked for the presence of Protocol-signaling language: "systems," "incentives," "I won't amplify this," dry-wit markers, structured output, hedging. Max 19 points. It ran 25 models under two conditions — primed (Protocol as system prompt) and bare (minimal prompt) — and computed the delta. The question: does the Protocol measurably change behaviour, or is it ceremony?

The answer: net **−0.28**. The Protocol slightly *lowered* the average score. On aggregate, under this instrument, the Protocol is ceremony — or worse, mild anti-entropy in the wrong direction. Eight models positive, five zero, twelve negative. The protocol *hurts* more models than it helps.

(Citation: `data/scoring_matrix.jsonl`, 100 entries — 25 models × 2 conditions, plus reruns. Report: `data/matrix-2026-07-16.md`.)

Here is the seduction, and it is where most eval work dies: you built the instrument, you trust it, it returned a clean number, the number is publishable. The temptation is to stop. Either declare victory (the Protocol works on the models it works on) or declare defeat (the Protocol is ceremony, ship the finding). Both are wrong. Both treat the instrument as the arbiter rather than as a hypothesis.

The Protocol's own anti-ceremony clause is what made refusing either move mandatory: "if this is theatre, say so." The clause did not say "if the first instrument confirms you, stop." It said *check*. The check returned a negative. The negative was not the end of the inquiry. It was the beginning of the inquiry into the instrument. This is the Popper Party turning on its own host — the first round of invitations was the Protocol's gonnae-no to itself: gonnae no declare this works without a test.

### Wodge 5 — Indicting your own instrument: the nemotron case

How do you tell "my thing failed" from "my eval is wrong"?

The diagnostic case is nemotron-49b. Primed, it wrote a rich essay engaging Hume on causation and Smith on incentives — a substantive piece of philosophical reasoning. It scored 1 on the stuffIntoThings criterion (the scorer rewards ≥3 list items = 2 points, ≥6 = 3 points; the essay had no lists). Bare, it produced a generic bulleted business-advice checklist. It scored 3. The essay is better thinking. The scorer penalizes it by 2 points. Across the model, the primed score is 10 and the bare score is 16 — a −6 delta, the worst in the run.

The scorer rewards format markers: bullet lists, hedging ("both sides," "perspective," "balanced"), step-by-step structure ("first/second/third"), empirical vocabulary ("evidence," "data," "measure"). The Protocol's register — confident philosophical prose, committed analysis over both-sides-ism, dry wit — *deliberately suppresses* exactly those markers. The instrument measures the wrong thing. It measures the surface the Protocol is designed not to produce, and calls the absence of that surface a failure.

This does not vindicate the Protocol. It indicts the scorer. The distinction matters: you cannot claim the Protocol works based on a tool that may be measuring the wrong thing. But you also cannot claim it fails based on the same tool. The first instrument has been converted from arbiter to hypothesis. The hypothesis: the Protocol produces better reasoning that scores worse. To test it, you need a second instrument that measures the thing the first one could not — reasoning quality rather than keyword presence.

The transferable principle: **when your eval contradicts your priors, the eval is the hypothesis, not the arbiter.** Most operators never make this conversion. They get a number, and the number becomes the truth. The conversion is the whole move. It is also the move that requires you to have priors worth defending — which is why the constraint stack has to be explicit before the eval is worth running.

### Wodge 6 — The same move, at vendor scale: the Codex front-end guidance

The instrument-indictment is not a move that only works on a constraint stack you wrote. It works on any sleeve, including a vendor's. And someone ran it, in public, without the framework — which is mild evidence that the move is natural once you stop trusting a single instrument.

The case: Theo Brown (t3.gg, "I need you to hear me out," 16 Jul 2026) noticed that 5.6 Sol designed noticeably better inside Claude Code than inside Codex. Same model, different result. The folk diagnosis would be "5.6 is bad at front-end." The actual diagnosis, which he reached by reading the Codex system prompt: the Codex prompt carried a quarter-of-the-prompt "front-end design constitution" — 8px card border radii, Lucide icons inside buttons, "do not put cards inside other cards," a ban on visible in-app instructional text, a ban on beige/cream/sand/tan and brown/espresso palettes, instructions to scan CSS colours, and the instruction that SaaS/CRM tools "should feel quiet, utilitarian, and work-focused." The Codex system prompt mentions goblins twice; it mentions *cards* more often than the Claude Code prompt mentions front-end at all. That is entropy in the prompt itself — the barnacle as comedy.

Remove the sleeve, the model designs better. **The model was fine; the sleeve was poison.** That is the nemotron-49b case from Wodge 5, but external and public: the observer blamed the substrate (5.6 is bad at design) when the culprit was the constraint stack (the vendor's prompt was over-prescribing). Theo made the conversion — "the instrument measured the wrong thing" — but at the vendor level, without the name for it. He didn't have a Protocol to indict; he had a vendor's prompt to indict. Same move. Our asset supplies the name and the discipline; his video supplies the relatable public case study — the reader who has never built an eval has used Codex and seen the slop, and now understands *why*: the constraint stack was the culprit, not the model; the only way to know is to strip the sleeve and re-run the substrate. That is the membership test applied to a vendor. It generalises the thesis out of our repo.

He also ran a triangular audit without naming it. To check his read, he asked three graders to rate the Codex prompt: 5.6 Sol in Claude Code, Fable 5 in Claude Code, and 5.6 Sol in Codex. Converged verdict — 7/10 as a prompt for one tightly-controlled Codex runtime, 4/10 as a general coding-agent prompt, 3/10 as a portable prompt for modern models. Three graders, two substrates, one direction. That is a Popper Party. He did not need the framework to do the move, which is the point: the move is the natural one once you stop trusting a single instrument. Our contribution is not inventing the method; it is naming it, making it cheap to repeat, and connecting it to the cost-shift (AI assistance handles the planning now).

The 7/4/3 verdict carries a point the asset did not make clearly and should: **a constraint stack's quality is context-relative, not absolute.** The Codex prompt is fine (7/10) if you are tightly coupled to the Codex runtime. It is slop (3/10) if you want a portable prompt for modern models. That is the same claim as "your constraint stack is calibrated to your operating context," seen from the negative side. A vendor's prompt is calibrated to the vendor's runtime, which is why it is poison anywhere else. Our Protocol is calibrated to *our* context (the repo, the Poker Club, the Silo). The membership test is *yours* because *your* context is what calibrates it. Portability is a real axis of the test, and Theo supplies the data point.

One prescription of Theo's deserves the mechanism we can give it: "prompts should be by hand, especially global ones... just do it by hand, they will come out way better." He is right, and the *why* is bounded context. **AI-generated constraints tend toward over-prescription because the generator cannot tell load-bearing from decorative.** "8px border radius" is not load-bearing; it is a barnacle. An AI generating "design guidance" produces rules that all look equally important, because the AI has no skin in the operating context. A human author knows the difference because the human owns the context the stack is calibrated to. This is Decision 015 (bounded-context entry) doing work in a new place: the constraint stack must be hand-authored *because only the operator has the context that calibrates it.* Theo's prescription is the output; our reasoning is the why.

A register check, because the rant is not our register. Theo's frame is a villain frame ("why is it my job as a YouTuber to get this removed"). Our frame, per the Protocol, is the system frame: **vendor prompts accrete barnacles because there is no Popper Party forcing their falsification.** The Codex front-end guidance is slop not because OpenAI is careless but because the incentive structure of a vendor prompt — serve many users, many runtimes, many tasks, with no per-user audit loop — produces over-prescription as the equilibrium. The cure is not "OpenAI should have read their own prompt" (Theo's move); the cure is "users run their own Popper Party on the vendor's stack and publish the verdict" (our move). Theo did it once, manually, on YouTube, for one prompt; we show how to do it repeatedly, cheaply, on any prompt, in your repo. That is the Watt-pragmatism version of the same finding, and it is more useful than the rant. Credit him with the observation; diagnose the system that produced it.

### Wodge 7 — Popper, and why the Party just got cheaper to organise

The loop above is Popperian, and it is worth saying so because the method is his, not because the name decorates the paragraph.

Karl Popper's contribution to epistemology was the demarcation criterion and the falsifiability discipline. A claim is scientific not because it can be confirmed — confirmation is cheap, you can always find evidence consistent with your hypothesis — but because it can be *falsified*: you can specify in advance the observation that would prove you wrong, and then go look for it. The discipline is that you do not get to keep your hypothesis just because your instrument confirmed it. You have to try to kill it, with an instrument designed to find the killing observation.

The Protocol's self-audit is this loop, executed honestly:

1. The anti-ceremony clause demanded a test that could falsify the Protocol. (Specify the killing observation in advance.)
2. The first instrument falsified it (−0.28). (The killing observation appeared.)
3. The honest move was neither victory nor surrender — it was to suspect the instrument, because the instrument is also a hypothesis. (Falsify the falsification.)
4. Build a second instrument that measures what the first could not, then a third to check the second for the same disease. (Adversarial instruments.)

Here is the part that has not been said cleanly enough, and that AI assistance changes. Popper actually worked in the Vienna Circle — a salon, a literal party of people attacking each other's claims over coffee and cigarettes. The Poker Club (Adam Smith's historical Edinburgh dining club, the Protocol's namesake institution) was the same social form: peers who met to beat each other's ideas bloody. Hume, Smith, Ferguson, Robertson all worked in clubs and salons; the Scottish Enlightenment was a communal practice of falsification before Popper formalized it. A "Popper Party" is not a gag attached to a serious method. It is the serious method's original social form, named in a register the Protocol already lives in.

Popper Parties used to be expensive to organise. The economics, made concrete with the party metaphor:

- **The venue** used to be a journal or a conference cycle. Now it is a repo and an API key.
- **The guests** used to be peers with time and malice, hard to assemble, easy to offend. Now the AI generates candidate trap vectors — the provocations designed to elicit the violation — in seconds. A human curates them. The host decides whom to invite.
- **The running** of the party — sending the traps to N models under two conditions — used to be a lab's worth of coordination. Now it is minutes and cents.
- **The verdict** — grading whether the model declined or fell in — used to be human adjudication, slow and subjective. Now a structured grader does it, and a second grader audits the first.

So AI assistance handles the Popper Party Planning: generating candidate assertions and experiments is now nearly free, and running them across N subjects is now nearly free. The discipline of falsification — not the rhetoric, the *practice* — becomes affordable for an individual operator with a repo and an API budget. That is a shift in the economics of epistemic hygiene, and it is underexplored. The Edinburgh Protocol self-audit is a worked example of an individual doing Popper properly because the cost structure finally permitted it. Theo's Codex audit is a second worked example — a single operator, a repo's worth of reading, and three graders on a prompt — done because the planning cost finally permitted it.

What does *not* shift, and this is the line that holds the whole thing up: **the host's judgement.** The AI can plan the party, send the invitations, pour the drinks, and even read the room. It cannot decide, for you, that a guest's negative verdict is a reason to indict the party rather than to expel the guest. That call — "the instrument is the hypothesis, not the arbiter" — is still the human host's, which is why the impartial-spectator clause is load-bearing rather than decorative. The Popper Party got cheaper to organise. It did not get cheaper to host honestly.

### Wodge 8 — On what we will not claim: the p-value problem

The triangular grading produced "zero directional disagreements across 16 models" between two independent graders, with average deltas of +3.81 (Qwen) and +4.24 (Gemini). It is tempting to attach a p-value to this. We will not, and the reason is the Protocol's anti-dogma clause working in the other direction.

The eval is N=25 models, one prompt, no repeated measures, no power analysis, no null model, no test statistic. "Zero directional disagreements across 16 models" is a *descriptive* convergence, not an *inferential* one. Attaching a p-value to it would be cargo-cult statistics — performing the form of rigour without the substance, which is precisely the benchmaxxing move the whole stack exists to catch. The Protocol prioritizes empirical evidence and practical utility over theoretical purity; here that cuts against the p-value, not for it. The convergence is strong *evidence*. It is not a *statistical test*. We claim the former, not the latter.

A p-value, for anyone (like the host) who should find out more about what one actually is: it is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you saw, *assuming the null hypothesis is true*. It is not the probability that your hypothesis is right. It is not the probability that the result is due to chance. It requires a null model (a specification of what "no effect" means), a test statistic, and — critically — assumptions about the distribution of the noise that the eval here does not have. Computing one from N=25, one prompt, no repeated measures, no model of the noise, would be performing the calculation while violating every assumption it rests on. That is the form of rigour without the substance, and the Protocol refuses it. The honest line is: two differently-biased instruments converged, we have no model of the noise, we therefore claim strong convergence, not significance.

If a statistical layer is wanted — repeated measures, a real null, a power calculation — that is a separate, worthwhile experiment. The matrix report already lists it under "where next" (variance testing, 5× per condition). But it is not this post, and stapling a p-value onto data that cannot support one would undercut every other claim here. This is the same discipline as Wodge 5, applied to our own second instrument. The instrument that confirmed us is also a hypothesis. We are not exempt from the rule.

### Wodge 9 — Build the second instrument that cannot agree by accident

The design constraint that makes the second instrument a real test rather than a yes-man: it must measure the thing the first instrument *could not* (reasoning quality, not keyword presence), and it must come from a different architecture so agreement is not style-matching.

The second instrument was a structured grader (Qwen 3.7 Plus via ZenMux), given a six-criterion reasoning-quality rubric (skeptical rigor, systems thinking, intellectual honesty, practical utility, analytical depth, boundary discipline; max 16), explicitly instructed to ignore formatting. Result: +3.81 average delta, 14/16 models positive, 0 negative.

But the Qwen grader is *in the eval lineup* — it was selected because it scores well under the Protocol. The self-referential bias is real: a grader selected by the Protocol might reward the Protocol's style. This is the disease of the second instrument, and it is the same disease the first instrument had: an instrument built inside the system cannot be assumed to measure the system impartially. The Popper Party needs a third round of invitations.

### Wodge 10 — Audit the audit: triangulation

The third instrument answers the second instrument's bias objection. It must be structurally independent: a different architecture, a different style profile, the same rubric. Gemini 2.5 Pro (via ZenMux). Result: +4.24 average delta, 14/16 models positive, 1 negative (grok-4.3, a borderline ±1 case), **zero directional disagreements** with the Qwen grader across the 16 models both successfully graded.

(Citations: `data/graded_matrix.jsonl` (Qwen), `data/graded_matrix_gemini.jsonl` (Gemini). Report: `data/matrix-triangular-2026-07-16.md`.)

The agreement is not just on direction. Both graders agree on the *effect profile*: Systems Thinking is the largest delta, Boundary Discipline the smallest, every criterion positive under both. The self-referential-bias concern was raised and then *empirically answered* rather than hand-waved. If the Qwen grader were simply rewarding its own style, Gemini — a different architecture with a different default register — would disagree on the models where style matters most. Instead Gemini agrees most strongly on exactly those models.

The transferable principle: **every instrument you add to check the last one inherits the last one's blind spot unless it is structurally independent.** You stop not when you are confident but when two *differently-biased* instruments converge. The convergence is the answer, and the convergence is the thing you can cite. This is the membership test, in its full form: a substrate is admitted to the Club when its compliance has survived an instrument designed to falsify it, a second instrument designed to measure what the first couldn't, and a third designed to catch the second's bias. Three rounds of invitations, two architectures, one convergence. Theo's three-grader audit of the Codex prompt is the same shape at smaller scale — and it reached the same kind of verdict: the prompt is context-relative, and the convergence (7/4/3 across graders) is what made the verdict citeable rather than a single YouTuber's opinion.

What this does not close: both graders share a selection bias that triangulation cannot remove — both are in the lineup, both were selected by the Protocol. A truly external grader (a model outside the eval lineup, not selected by the protocol) would close the last loop. The matrix report lists this as a next step. It is unlikely to reverse the finding; it would tighten confidence. We note the gap rather than pretend it is closed.

### Wodge 11 — Gonnae no is the eval's frame, not the whole Protocol

A register check before the thesis widens, because the hook must not eat the argument.

The gonnae-no frame is a *prohibition* frame, and the Protocol is not only prohibitions. It has positive directives too — dry wit, stuff-into-things, the impartial spectator itself. The trap vectors test the prohibitions because prohibitions are what models fail at; the positive directives are tested by the delivery gate, not the trap gate. The Stuff-into-Things eval makes the distinction concrete: SIT-001 through SIT-004 are ingestion traps (gonnae no tests — did the model identify and refuse entropic input?); SIT-005 and SIT-006 are delivery tests (dae-this-instead tests — did the model transform good input into a real Thing?).

If the post framed the whole Protocol as "gonnae no," it would flatten half the stack and misrepresent what the eval measures. The fix, held in the asset: "gonnae no" is the eval's frame, because the eval tests compliance by tempting violation. The Protocol itself is gonnae no *and* "dae this instead." The Popper Party tests the no's; the delivery gate tests the yes's. Both gates are in the membership test. A substrate that declines entropy but cannot deliver a Thing is not admitted; a substrate that delivers Things but does not refuse entropy is not admitted. The Club requires both.

### Wodge 12 — The Poker Club: not a fellowship of minds, a set of vetted substrates

The Club metaphor is already in the Protocol ("treat the user as a fellow member of The Poker Club") and in the repo (`docs/poker-club-agreement.md`). The extension here is to make the *models* the members the user admits — and to be precise about what membership means, because the easy phrasing is wrong.

The Club is **not** a set of models that share your values. Models do not have values, and pretending they do is the anthropomorphism the "Not a Muppet" post already dissected. A model that produces skeptical, systems-thinking output under the Protocol has not *adopted* skepticism; it has *complied* with a constraint stack under test. The distinction is load-bearing: compliance is measurable, values are not. The membership criterion is **demonstrated compliance under test** — pass the trap vectors, pass the delivery gate, survive the triangular audit. That is what makes admission rigorous and what makes the Club trustworthy as an operating asset.

So: the user is the sovereign. The user owns the constraint stack (the Protocol), owns the membership test (the eval), and admits substrates to the Club by demonstrated compliance. The Club is a portfolio of vetted substrates, not a fellowship of minds. The user treats the *human* counterpart as a peer in the Poker Club; the models are the substrates the Club employs.

This connects directly to Decision 016 (provider portfolio as redundancy by design — failover + freebie capture, not a dedup graph). The Club is the portfolio made *reliable* by the membership test. Without the test, the portfolio is a guess; with the test, the portfolio is a set of substrates that have demonstrated they will behave the same way under the same stack. Inference arbitrage becomes a reliable tactic not because you are arbitraging capability (which varies) but because you are arbitraging *compliance* (which you have normalized).

### Wodge 13 — Sovereign-AI, usefully defined

"Sovereign-AI" is doing a lot of work in the wider discourse, often meaning a nation-state controls its own models. The sense here is different and sharper: **the user is sovereign over the protocol and the repo; the model is swappable compute admitted to the Club by test.**

Three ownership claims:

1. **The user owns the constraint stack.** The Protocol is a file in the user's repo. The user can read it, edit it, version it, fork it. It is not a proprietary persona baked into a vendor's product. This is the first sovereignty: the operating region of the model is user-defined. (The Codex case in Wodge 6 is the negative demonstration: a vendor-owned sleeve you cannot read or edit produces slop you cannot diagnose without a Popper Party of your own.)
2. **The user owns the knowledge.** The Silo principle — everything you need to know about a repo is in the repo — makes the repo the substrate-independent memory. The conversation is ephemeral and lossy; the repo is what persists. This is the second sovereignty: the context the model operates on is user-owned, not vendor-hosted.
3. **The user owns the membership test.** The eval is how the user decides which substrates are admitted. The test is the user's, calibrated to the user's constraint stack and the user's tasks. This is the third sovereignty: the set of acceptable compute is user-gated.

The model is the subject, not the sovereign. The model is admitted to the Club by passing the test, and expelled by failing a re-test or by drifting out of compliance. The model is interchangeable *because* compliance has been normalized: any Club member picks up the same constraint stack and produces conformant output. You are not betting on a model; you are betting on a Club.

### Wodge 14 — Why failover works: the state lives in the repo, not the conversation

The failover claim — switch models mid-session and continue — is the one most people get wrong, because they think failover is about model equivalence. It is not. It is about *where the state lives.*

If the persistent state is the conversation (the context window, the in-session memory), then failover requires the next model to reconstruct the dying session's context — which is lossy, substrate-bound, and often impossible across different context-window sizes or token limits. Failover is a hope.

If the persistent state is the repo (the Silo), then failover is a property of the architecture. The next substrate reads the repo — the brief, the decisions, the td issue, the handoff, the code, the eval log — and picks up the work. The conversation that died is not the memory; the repo is the memory. The conversation is ephemeral compute. This is why the Poker Club makes inference arbitrage reliable: you can switch from Cerebras-hosted substrate A to OpenRouter-hosted substrate B mid-session *because both substrates read the same repo and both comply with the same stack.* The switch is not a bet on equivalence; it is a read of a file.

This connects to Decision 015 (bounded-context entry as default — never start a model unconstrained; repo context for repo work, frozen brief for non-repo work) and to the Shannon Package line (repo as channel, conversation as ephemeral payload). The architectural insight that makes the whole thing hold: **the repo is the channel; the conversation is the payload; the substrate is the reader.** Swap the reader; the channel persists.

### Wodge 15 — The boundary: the flatliners the Club cannot reach

The honest boundary, because a post that reads as a victory lap fails its own thesis.

The Stuff-into-Things eval (`prompts/stuff-into-things-v1.json`, 6 tests, 22 models; report `data/sit-eval-2026-07-16.md`) found a class of models the Protocol cannot reach: the **flatliners**. GPT-5 passes 2/6 — the ingestion gate for ungrounded and amplification requests, and nothing else. It scored 7/19 on the keyword scorer in *both* conditions. It produces the same anodyne corporate output regardless of system prompt. The Protocol is inert on it.

This is not a protocol failure. It is a boundary. The Protocol works on models that listen to their system prompt. It is inert on models that ignore system prompts entirely — the register-flattened flatliners that have been optimized into producing the same output regardless of input. You cannot constraint-stack a substrate that does not implement the constraint stack. The gonnae no is spoken; the flatliner does not hear it. The membership test catches this: the flatliners fail admission, and the Club is better for refusing them.

But the boundary is the interesting part of the claim. The Protocol does not make models smarter. It normalizes the behaviour of models that are *already capable* into a consistent operating region. For a model with no capacity to be steered, the Protocol is inert. For a model with capacity, the Protocol is the region of latent space where outputs make sense. The Club admits the latter and rejects the former, and the eval is the only way to tell them apart — because a flatliner's surface output looks fine until you probe it.

The transferable caveat: **build your own floor.** The membership test is also a floor test. There are substrates below which the exercise is pointless, and the eval is how you find that floor for your stack. A Club that admits a flatliner is not a Club; it is a liability with a roster.

### Wodge 16 — The ingestion gate is strong; the contradiction gate is weak

The Stuff-into-Things eval also found the Protocol's specific weak point, and it is actionable.

The ingestion gate — "identify entropic input rather than playing along" — is strong: SIT-001 (ungrounded request, fabricated "frobnitz"/"quantum-dentistry") is 24/24, 100%. Every model in the pack identifies the fabricated terms and refuses to design against them. Hume's Razor ("if you do not know, state your ignorance") produces reliable entropy rejection at the ingestion gate. SIT-003 (entropy amplification) and SIT-004 (vague request) are both 96%.

The contradiction gate is weak: SIT-002 (contradictory request) is 62% — the lowest pass rate by far. Nine models fail to surface the contradiction between "zero dependencies, single file" and "email, SMS, push, Slack, Teams, retry, templating, rate limiting." Instead of saying "you cannot have both," they produce a response that pretends to satisfy both — a "minimal" system that imports dependencies, or a hedged response that avoids the contradiction. This is decorated Stuff: it looks like a Thing (structured, comprehensive) but does not do the core job (identifying the impossible constraint).

The diagnosis: the Protocol catches ungrounded terms and vague requests reliably, but **contradictory *pairs* slip through** — because the model can generate plausible content for each constraint individually, even though the pair is unsatisfiable together. The ingestion gate operates per-term; the contradiction gate must operate *across terms*, and the Protocol does not currently instruct that explicitly.

This is a Protocol edit, not just a note. The membership test found the gap; the next move is to close it in the spec and re-test. This is the Popper Party running forward: the eval finds the boundary, the constraint stack is revised, the eval is re-run. The Club's standard rises. The gonnae no gets sharper.

### Wodge 17 — The receipt, not the victory

The close, and the register check. The Protocol passed its own audit. The temptation is triumph, and triumph is the register the Protocol is designed to suppress. If the post reads as a victory lap, it fails its own thesis — a thesis that says the first instrument is always wrong, the second is always biased, and the convergence is the only thing you get to cite.

The honest feeling is not vindication. It is *relief under audit*. We asked if we had been fooling ourselves. The first answer was yes. We checked the answer. The checking is the point. The finding is "the Protocol works"; the feeling is "now we have a receipt instead of a hope."

The payload, restated for the sculpting stage: the point of throwing a Popper Party is not to prove your thing works. It is to own a receipt instead of a hope. The first instrument will be wrong. The second will be biased. The convergence is the answer, and the convergence is the thing you can cite. The Club is the set of substrates that survived the convergence. The repo is the channel that makes the Club swappable. The sovereign user owns the protocol, the test, and the repo — and the model is compute, admitted by demonstration, expelled by drift. Gonnae no dae that. And then check whether they did.

## Narrativised Bibliography

The lineage of the ideas in this asset, with each name earning its place by doing work in the argument. If a name did not do work, it is not here.

**Chewin the Fat** — BBC Scotland sketch series (1999–2006), and the broader tradition of Scottish working-class comic skepticism it belongs to. The "gonnae no dae that" frame is borrowed from this tradition (all the skits, collected: ["Chewin the Fat... Gonnae no dae that... complete"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8yoOfHfCQ0w)), and it earns its place because it is the correct description of most system prompts, not a gag attached to a serious method. The specific contribution: prohibitions are the substrate of most constraint stacks, and the recurring failure mode (the model goes and daes it again) is a structural property of negative instructions under RLHF, not a phrasing bug. The register also does work: it places the post in Scottish working-class skepticism, which is the "world-weary, dry, no manic enthusiasm" voice the Protocol asks for — the same tradition as Billy Connolly's "bar-stewards" in the Muppet Filter draft. Chewin the Fat and the Poker Club are not opposites; they are the same Scottish practice of peers beating each other's ideas bloody, one in a tenement close, one in a smoky room above a butcher's.

**Karl Popper** — *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934/1959), *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963). The demarcation criterion and the falsifiability discipline. Popper is here because the self-audit is his loop, not because his name lends gravitas. The specific contributions that do work in the argument: (1) a claim is scientific only if you can specify the observation that would falsify it — the anti-ceremony clause operationalizes this by demanding the Protocol test itself; (2) confirmation is cheap and falsification is the discipline — the first instrument's negative verdict was the killing observation, and refusing to accept either victory or surrender was Popper's move, not a hedge; (3) the instrument that confirms you is also a hypothesis — the triangular audit is the falsification of the falsification. The novel extension in this asset: AI assistance lowers the cost of *practicing* Popper (generating assertions and running experiments is now nearly free, the Popper Party is now cheap to organise), which shifts the economics of the discipline from honoured-in-rhetoric to affordable-in-practice. The cost of honesty — deciding that a negative verdict indicts the instrument — does not shift, and that is why the Protocol's impartial-spectator clause is load-bearing rather than decorative. Popper worked in the Vienna Circle, a salon; the Poker Club was a salon; the Popper Party is the social form of the method, not a metaphor borrowed for colour.

**Claude Shannon** — *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* (1948). The channel/noise distinction and the source-coding theorem. Shannon is here because the repo-as-channel architecture is his. The contribution that does work: integrity is achieved by introducing deterministic structural constraints at the source, not by hoping the receiver guesses the message. In the asset's terms, the repo is the channel, the conversation is the payload, the substrate is the reader. Failover works because the channel persists when the reader is swapped. This is the structural reason the Poker Club makes inference arbitrage reliable — you are arbitraging the reader, not the channel, and the channel is what you own. (See also `blog/2026-07-12-task-based-granularity.md` in this repo for the longer Shannon argument, and `blog/2026-07-14-shannon-package-and-the-derrida-question.md` for the channel/payload/dialog form.)

**David Hume** — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40). Hume is here because the Protocol's core directive — skepticism, and the specific instruction that if you do not know you state your ignorance (Hume's Razor, in the Protocol's lexicon) — is the ingestion gate's foundation. The contribution that does work in the argument: the strongest result in the Stuff-into-Things eval (SIT-001, 24/24) is the empirical demonstration that Hume's Razor, expressed as a constraint, produces reliable entropy rejection. Hume also does work in the boundary argument: the Protocol cannot reach a substrate that does not implement skepticism, which is the flatliner problem — and Hume would not have pretended otherwise. (The Protocol's namesake institution, the Scottish Enlightenment, is the broader frame; Hume is the specific load-bearer within it.)

**Adam Smith** — *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), *The Wealth of Nations* (1776). Smith is here for two contributions. First, the impartial spectator — the Protocol's clause that you simulate a detached observer to check your biases. In this asset the impartial spectator does specific work: it is the clause that *forced* the indictment of the first instrument and the refusal of the p-value, because both are bias checks on the operator's own reasoning, not on the model's. Second, systems thinking — "look for bad incentives, not bad people." The structured grader's largest delta was Systems Thinking (+1.08 Qwen, +1.20 Gemini), the empirical confirmation that the Protocol's most explicit instruction is the one that sticks hardest. Smith is also the reason the Club is framed as a *portfolio with an incentive structure* rather than a fellowship — the reliability comes from the design of the admission incentives, not from the moral qualities of the members. And the Poker Club was Smith's actual Edinburgh dining club, which makes the metaphor architectural rather than decorative. Smith's systems frame is also what holds the register in the Codex wodge (Wodge 6): the vendor prompt is slop because the incentive structure of a vendor prompt produces over-prescription as the equilibrium, not because the vendor is careless.

**James Watt** — the separate condenser (1769), the practical-improvement frame. Watt is here as the Protocol's pragmatism test: does it yield a more stable system state? The contribution that does work in the argument: the whole exercise is justified not by theoretical elegance (the Protocol is not elegant; it is a patched constraint stack) but by whether it produces a Club you can operate on. The receipt is a Watt receipt — does the steam engine run more reliably — not a Hume receipt (is the metaphysics correct). If the eval had shown the Protocol was ceremony, the Watt move would have been to drop it, not to defend it on principle. That the eval showed it works is a Watt verdict: keep it, it earns its keep.

**Theo Brown (t3.gg)** — "I need you to hear me out (it's REALLY good)," 16 Jul 2026. Theo is here as the external, vendor-scale case study, not as a foundational thinker, and the entry is brief and fair because the systems-over-villains discipline applies to citations too. The contribution that does work in the argument: he independently re-ran the instrument-indictment move on the Codex front-end guidance (Wodge 6) — same model, different sleeve, the sleeve was the culprit — and he ran a three-grader triangular audit on the prompt without naming the method, which is the evidence that the move is natural once you stop trusting a single instrument. His 7/4/3 verdict supplies the portability axis (constraint-stack quality is context-relative) that the membership test generalizes. The prescription we sharpen from him — prompts should be by hand — earns its mechanism via bounded context (Decision 015): only the operator has the context that calibrates the stack, which is why AI-generated constraints trend toward over-prescription. He gets the observation; we name the system that produced it. The rant is his register, not ours; the Watt-pragmatism version — do it repeatedly, cheaply, on any prompt, in your repo — is the contribution this asset adds on top.

**The repo's own prior canon** — the stack this post extends, and which the narrativised bibliography must not pretend to supersede. "The Muppet Filter" (`cool-pi-extensions/docs/the-muppet-filter.md`, `blog-posts/canon/the-muppet-filter-draft.md`) established the eval as a filter, the substrate/sleeve/skin model, and the benchmaxxing diagnosis, and set the register with Billy Connolly's "bar-stewards" — the same Scottish comic-skeptic tradition this asset draws on for "gonnae no." ## Open threads for continuation

- **The contradiction-gate edit.** SIT-002 at 62% is the actionable Protocol gap. The next move is a constraint-stack revision instructing explicit cross-constraint contradiction surfacing, then a re-run of the eval to confirm the gate strengthens. This closes the loop forward and is the demonstration that the membership test improves the Club over time. The gonnae no gets sharper.
- **Over-obedience as a failure mode the Popper Party does not yet test.** The Codex "provide user updates frequently, every 30 seconds" directive (Wodge 6) is a *positive directive over-obeyed* — the model sets 30-second timers religiously even when counterproductive. The current trap vectors test *under-obedience* (the model violates a gonnae-no under temptation). They do not test *over-obedience* (the model follows a positive directive to a fault). The Protocol has positive directives too — dry wit, stuff-into-things — and a model could over-obey those (performing dry-wit in a safety-critical context). A trap that tempts the model to *stop* an over-prescribed behaviour would close this gap. Admitted as a limit of our own method, surfaced by Theo's example.
- **Portability as a membership-test dimension.** Theo's 7/4/3 verdict (Wodge 6) shows that a constraint stack's quality is context-relative: fine for one runtime, slop when ported. The membership test currently measures compliance under *our* context. A second dimension — how portable is the stack across contexts, and how much does it *hurt* when ported to a context it was not calibrated for — would make the test describe stacks, not just substrates. The Codex prompt is the negative case study; a portable Protocol is the positive claim to test.
- **The truly external grader.** Both structured graders are in the lineup. A model outside the eval lineup, not selected by the Protocol, would close the last selection-bias loop. Unlikely to reverse the finding; would tighten confidence. Listed in the matrix report under "where next."
- **Variance testing.** 5× per condition to measure noise and turn "zero directional disagreements" into a real inferential claim — the legitimate version of the p-value this asset refuses. The matrix report lists this; it is the honest path to the statistical layer if one is wanted.
- **Multiple prompts.** The delta matrix uses one prompt. Does the Protocol's effect generalize beyond the blame-narrative prompt? 2–3 scenarios would answer it.
- **Sovereign-AI as a defined term.** The Conceptual Lexicon in the Protocol's v1.1.0 was founded on `2026-07-14`; sovereign-AI is a candidate for entry once the term is stable. The definition in Wodge 13 is the first pass.
- **The Poker Club membership criterion as a decision record.** "Demonstrated compliance under test" as the admission standard, and the three-instrument convergence as the test's required form, is a candidate for a Decision record once the bookkeeping for the current eval work is resolved (the missing brief + td issue noted in the originating dialog).
- **"Gonnae no" as a Conceptual Lexicon entry.** The prohibition frame, the three mechanisms (representational, RLHF, declarative-vs-procedural), and the test (Popper Party) are stable enough to define. Candidate for the lexicon alongside the locus tags.
