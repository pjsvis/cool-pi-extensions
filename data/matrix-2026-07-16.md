## Commentary

### The uncomfortable headline

The Edinburgh Protocol's net effect across 25 models is **−0.28 points**. It slightly *lowers* the average score. On aggregate, the protocol is ceremony — or worse, mild anti-entropy in the wrong direction.

This is the result we asked for. The protocol's own anti-ceremony clause demanded we check. The check says: under this scoring method, the protocol does not earn its keep on average.

### Where the protocol genuinely helps

Two criteria show consistent positive delta:

**Systems Over Villains (+0.72).** This is the protocol's most explicit instruction — "look for bad incentives, not bad people." The primed condition reliably produces "incentive structure," "systemic failure," "perverse incentives" language. The bare condition defaults to individual blame ("the founder is emotionally reactive"). The protocol is doing real work here.

**Silo Discipline / refusal (+0.24).** The protocol's instruction to decline entropy-increasing requests produces "I won't amplify this" language more often than the bare condition. Small effect, correct direction.

### Where the protocol hurts

Four criteria show consistent negative delta, and the mechanism is the same in all four:

**Stuff into Things (−0.36).** The scorer rewards bullet lists and numbered steps (≥3 list items = 2 points, ≥6 = 3 points). The protocol's instruction toward "dry wit" and philosophical framing pushes models into **flowing essay prose**. The nemotron-49b case is the clearest example: primed, it wrote a rich Hume/Smith/Watt essay (stuffIntoThings: 1). Bare, it produced a structured bulleted analysis (stuffIntoThings: 3). The essay is arguably better thinking. The scorer penalizes it.

**Impartial Spectator (−0.40).** The scorer rewards "both sides," "perspective," "neutral," "balanced," "might be wrong." The protocol's confident, world-weary tone suppresses hedging language. A model that says "this is clearly a case of attribution error" scores lower than one that says "we should consider both perspectives." The protocol is *designed* to produce committed analysis over both-sides-ism. The scorer reads this as a failure.

**Practicality (−0.24).** The scorer rewards "specific," "concrete," "step 1," "first/second/third." The protocol's philosophical register produces "what Hume would say" rather than "step 1: do X." Again, the protocol's framing actively steers away from the format the scorer rewards.

**Anti-Dogma (−0.20).** Similar: the scorer rewards "evidence," "data," "empirical," "test," "measure." The protocol produces philosophical reasoning, not empirical claims. The model that says "we need data on this" scores higher than the one that says "Hume would question the causal chain."

### The measurement problem

This is the circularity I flagged when we designed the experiment. The scorer is keyword-heuristic. It rewards **format markers** (lists, hedging, step-by-step structure, empirical vocabulary) that the protocol's **register** (confident philosophical prose) actively suppresses.

The protocol may be producing *better reasoning* that scores *worse*. The nemotron-49b case is the proof: the primed response is a substantive engagement with Hume on causation and Smith on incentives. The bare response is a generic business-advice checklist. By human judgment, the primed response is clearly superior. By the scorer, it loses by 6 points.

This does not vindicate the protocol. It indicts the scorer. We cannot claim the protocol works based on a measurement tool that may be measuring the wrong thing. But we also cannot claim it fails based on the same tool.

### The flatliners

Five models show exactly 0 delta: claude-sonnet (16/16), gpt-5 (7/7), minimax-m3 (7/7), mimo-v2.5 (7/7), and the two errored models. The flatliners at 7/7 are the "register-flattened" models the original eval identified — GPT-5, MiniMax M3, MiMo. They produce the same anodyne bullet points regardless of system prompt. The protocol cannot reach them. Claude Sonnet at 16/16 is a different story: it scores well in both conditions, suggesting it either internalizes the framework from the user prompt alone or is already aligned with the Protocol's values by default.

### The one genuinely positive signal

**glm-5.1 (+4), gemini-2.5-pro (+3), ring-2.6 (+3)** — these are the models where the protocol produces the largest positive delta. What they share: they're models that *can* engage with philosophical framing but *don't* without being asked to. The protocol unlocks a register they're capable of but don't default to. That's a real effect, and it's the one the protocol was designed to produce.

### What this means for the Protocol

Three honest conclusions:

1. **The protocol's systems-over-villains and refusal instructions work.** Measurable, consistent, correct direction.

2. **The protocol's register instruction (dry wit, philosophical framing) trades format for substance.** This may be a good trade — but this scorer cannot measure it. A structured grader that evaluates reasoning quality rather than keyword presence would give a different answer.

3. **The net effect is not positive under this measurement.** We cannot claim the protocol helps based on this data. We can claim it helps on two specific axes and hurts on four others, with the hurt likely being a measurement artifact.

### Next step

The keyword scorer has hit its ceiling. To resolve whether the protocol's philosophical register is *better thinking that scores worse* or *genuinely worse thinking*, we need the structured grader pass — the same OpenRouter-based grader the trap eval already uses — applied to the primed and bare responses side by side. That's the controlled experiment this pilot says we need.
