# On Literary Adaptation and the Question of What Films Break

*A conversation about why some books can't be filmed, and the ones that almost got away with it.*

---

## The Problem with Don DeLillo

*White Noise*, *Underworld*, *Zero K* — they look filmable and they're not.

The interior is the thing. The consciousness. The paranoia. The slow-motion collapse of consensus reality. The camera can't get there.

The films that exist are fine-ish. They capture the surface. The texture escapes.

DeLillo writes about the way things feel, the way meaning drains out of objects, the way language fails when you need it most. These are not visual problems. They are experiential problems.

You can't film the experience of reading *White Noise*. You can film the plot, which is about a man whose wife is stealing drugs and there's a train crash and someone might die. But the book isn't about that. The book is about the fear of death and the way consumerism fills the gap and the way language becomes advertising and the way nothing quite adds up.

The camera shows you the train crash. It doesn't show you the terror.

---

## On Pynchon

Paul Thomas Anderson did *Inherent Vice* well. He understood the stoner-noir tone, the conspiracy-without-resolution, the way nothing quite adds up.

That was the right director for Pynchon — someone who could hold the ambiguity without forcing it to resolve. The book is a mess. The film is a beautiful mess. The tone is preserved. The confusion is the point.

The wrong directors try to make Pynchon make sense. They resolve the conspiracy, tie up the loose ends, explain what happened. This destroys the book. The conspiracy is supposed to be incomprehensible. The mystery is the meaning.

## The Calvino Problem

*If on a winter's night a traveler* is literally unfilmable.

It's about reading. About the experience of the reader. About the recursion of story-within-story, the endless beginning, the narrator who addresses you directly, the way the text keeps breaking and starting again.

You can't put that on screen without destroying it. The whole point is that it's a book, that you're reading it, that the experience is literary. The moment you see characters on a screen, the recursion collapses. The meta breaks.

Calvino knew this. He wrote the book as a puzzle about reading itself. It works because it's a book.

---

## Borges and the Aleph

Borges has the same issue. The labyrinth is conceptual. The Aleph is about seeing everything at once — the entire universe in one point, every moment simultaneously — which is a visual idea that can't actually be visualized.

The stories work because you *imagine* them. You picture the library, the endless hexagonal rooms, the books that contain all possible books. The imagination does it better than the screen can.

You could film a man walking through a library. You cannot film the experience of understanding what the library means.

---

## The BBC Thing

The BBC adaptations of Borges stories actually work. They picked the right ones — short stories with more plot than meditation, pieces where the concept can be screen-shaped.

*The Garden of Forking Paths* has a spy story inside a philosophical puzzle. You can film the spy story. You can hint at the philosophy.

*The Aleph* is harder, but the BBC version tried. It doesn't quite work — the visualization of the Aleph is always going to be less than the concept — but it comes closer than a Hollywood version would.

The BBC understands something the studios don't: constraint. Take the right story, understand what can be adapted, trim the rest. It's the same reason the BBC Dickens works — they respect the source material by understanding what the source material is actually doing.

---

## What Doesn't Work

The DeLillo problem. The Pynchon problem when done wrong. The Calvino problem — nobody's cracked it, and they won't, because the text is about the experience of reading and reading is not filmable.

The experimental bloke in the alt-cinema space might be the one to watch. Someone working in that Antonioni/Cassavetes register — the thing that looks broken but holds together, that doesn't force resolution, that lets the ambiguity breathe.

But nobody has done Calvino. Nobody has done Borges well, not really. The Borges stories are too conceptual, too literary, too much about the experience of reading rather than the events being described.
